TL;DR

Following years of unprecedented expansion to combat economic crises, the world's major central banks are now engaged in the delicate and historically significant task of shrinking their balance sheets. This process, known as quantitative tightening (QT), marks a pivotal shift in global monetary policy. A recent workshop hosted by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York brought together leading economists and policymakers to dissect the challenges, risks, and long-term implications of this 'great unwinding.' The discussions focused on how to withdraw liquidity from the financial system without triggering instability, fundamentally reshaping the operational landscape of monetary policy for the foreseeable future.

Key Data Points

  • Central banks are actively reducing their balance sheets, which swelled to historic levels due to quantitative easing (QE) during the 2008 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • The process, known as quantitative tightening (QT), primarily involves letting assets like government bonds mature without reinvesting the proceeds, thereby removing money from the financial system.
  • A key challenge is determining the optimal future size of the balance sheet and the appropriate level of bank reserves needed to maintain financial stability in a post-QE world.
  • Policymakers are proceeding with caution to avoid repeating past disruptions, such as the September 2019 repo market turmoil, which signaled that reserves had fallen too low.
  • The unwinding process has significant global implications, affecting government borrowing costs, market liquidity, and the overall stance of monetary policy in the fight against inflation.

Deep Dive

The core of the challenge lies in the operational shift from a 'scarce reserves' to an 'ample reserves' framework for implementing monetary policy. Before 2008, central banks kept bank reserves scarce to control short-term interest rates. Post-crisis QE flooded the system with reserves, creating an 'ample' or 'abundant' reserves system, often called a 'floor system.' Now, as central banks unwind their balance sheets, they are effectively draining these reserves. The central question explored at the New York Fed workshop is how far this draining can go. Policymakers are trying to find the 'lowest comfortable level of reserves'—enough to prevent money market volatility but not so much as to be considered excessive stimulus. This involves complex modeling and real-time monitoring of money market indicators to avoid a 'cliff edge' where liquidity suddenly evaporates.

Different central banks are adopting varied approaches, reflecting their unique economic circumstances and market structures. The U.S. Federal Reserve, for instance, has primarily used a passive 'runoff' strategy, setting monthly caps on the amount of Treasury and mortgage-backed securities that mature without being replaced. This is seen as a predictable and gradual approach. Other central banks are contemplating or have used different tools. The discussions highlight a global learning process, where the experiences of one institution inform the strategies of others. The composition of the balance sheet is also a critical topic; for example, what is the ideal mix of short-term and long-term government debt for a central bank to hold in the long run? The answers will define the future footprint of central banks in financial markets.

Implications

The most direct implication of QT is the tightening of financial conditions. By reducing the demand for government bonds, central banks put upward pressure on longer-term interest rates, which translates into higher borrowing costs for governments, corporations, and households. This complements the use of policy rate hikes to control inflation but also carries the risk of slowing economic growth more than intended. For governments, the absence of the central bank as a major, consistent buyer of their debt means they must find new buyers, potentially at higher yields, which could strain public finances.

Financial stability is a paramount concern. The unwinding process is a journey into uncharted territory, and a misstep could have severe consequences. If a central bank withdraws too much liquidity too quickly, it could trigger sharp spikes in overnight lending rates and destabilize crucial funding markets, as witnessed in the U.S. in 2019. Such an event could force a sudden halt or even a reversal of QT, undermining the central bank's credibility. Therefore, policymakers are emphasizing a data-dependent approach, communicating their plans clearly, and building tools like the Fed's Standing Repo Facility (SRF) to act as a backstop against market stress, ensuring the unwinding process remains orderly.

Contrarian View

While the consensus focuses on the necessity and risks of shrinking central bank balance sheets, a contrarian perspective argues that the obsession with 'normalization' is misplaced. This view posits that a large balance sheet is not inherently problematic and may be a permanent feature of modern central banking in an 'ample reserves' system. Proponents suggest that a large, stable balance sheet can enhance financial stability by providing a plentiful supply of the safest asset (central bank reserves), reducing the likelihood of private sector funding panics. From this viewpoint, the primary tool for monetary policy should be the short-term policy rate, and the balance sheet should be viewed as a passive instrument for ensuring market liquidity, not an active tool to be constantly adjusted.

FAQ

  • What is quantitative tightening (QT)? Quantitative tightening is a monetary policy tool used by central banks to decrease the amount of money in the economy. It is the reverse of quantitative easing (QE) and involves the central bank reducing the size of its balance sheet by selling assets or letting them mature without reinvestment.
  • Why did central bank balance sheets become so large? Central bank balance sheets expanded dramatically as a result of large-scale asset purchase programs, known as quantitative easing (QE). These programs were implemented to combat major economic downturns, primarily the 2008 Global Financial Crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic, by lowering long-term interest rates and injecting liquidity into the financial system.
  • What are the main risks associated with unwinding these large balance sheets? The primary risk is inadvertently tightening financial conditions too much or too quickly, which could slow the economy excessively or trigger instability in financial markets. Specifically, reducing bank reserves to a level that is too low could cause a spike in short-term borrowing costs and disrupt market functioning, as seen in the U.S. repo market in September 2019.
