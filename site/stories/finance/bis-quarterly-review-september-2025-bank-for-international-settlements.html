TL;DR

The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) September 2025 Quarterly Review paints a sobering picture of a global financial system navigating a complex and uncertain landscape. Released against a backdrop of divergent monetary policies and persistent geopolitical tensions, the report highlights the emergence of powerful new forces reshaping risk. The key findings focus on the dual-edged nature of artificial intelligence in finance and the ever-expanding influence of non-bank financial intermediaries (NBFIs), often termed the 'shadow banking' sector. While central banks have managed to curb the worst of the post-pandemic inflation, the report suggests the next chapter of financial stability will be defined by the ability of regulators to get ahead of these rapidly evolving, technology-driven structural shifts. The review serves as a critical call to action for policymakers to address vulnerabilities that have quietly grown to systemic importance.

Key Data Points

  • AI as a Systemic Risk: The BIS warns that while generative AI is boosting productivity, its widespread adoption in finance creates risks of 'model monocultures' and opaque algorithmic decision-making that could amplify market shocks.
  • The Non-Bank Nexus: Non-bank financial intermediaries (NBFIs) now dominate crucial credit markets, but their lighter regulation and high leverage pose significant financial stability concerns, particularly in a sustained higher interest rate environment.
  • Divergent Monetary Paths: Major central banks are charting different courses on interest rates in response to varied domestic conditions, creating significant cross-currency volatility and placing acute pressure on emerging market economies.
  • Financial Fragmentation Deepens: The report quantifies the rising economic costs of geopolitical tensions, citing reduced cross-border capital flows, the splintering of payment systems, and increased friction in global finance.
  • Wholesale CBDCs Gain Traction: Central banks are accelerating work on wholesale Central Bank Digital Currencies (wCBDCs) to enhance the efficiency of cross-border payments, while progress on retail CBDCs remains cautious due to unresolved privacy and policy questions.

Deep Dive

The September 2025 review dedicates a significant chapter to what it terms the 'AI Conundrum'. The Bank for International Settlements acknowledges the profound efficiency gains AI offers, from hyper-personalized risk assessments and automated compliance to advanced algorithmic trading. However, the analysis pivots to the largely unaddressed systemic risks. A primary concern is the emergence of a 'model monoculture,' where a small number of dominant, third-party AI models are used across the industry for critical functions like credit scoring and asset valuation. This concentration risk means that a flaw or bias in a single underlying model could trigger correlated, system-wide failures. Furthermore, the report highlights the 'black box' problem, where the complexity of these AI systems makes their decision-making processes opaque even to their creators, let alone regulators. This opacity challenges effective supervision and makes it difficult to diagnose and contain AI-driven market anomalies, such as flash crashes, before they cascade through the interconnected financial system.

Another core theme is the continued and unchecked expansion of the non-bank financial intermediation (NBFI) sector. The BIS data indicates that private credit funds, hedge funds, and other NBFIs now represent the primary source of financing in several key corporate and real estate markets, eclipsing traditional banks. This shift, which accelerated after post-2008 regulations constrained bank balance sheets, has introduced a new nexus of risk. The report expresses concern over three specific vulnerabilities: significant hidden leverage, often achieved through complex derivatives; severe liquidity mismatches, where funds promise daily liquidity to investors while holding illiquid assets; and deep, often unmapped, interconnectedness with the core banking system. In a world of higher-for-longer interest rates, these vulnerabilities are magnified. The BIS cautions that a shock originating within the NBFI sector—such as a large fund failure—could rapidly propagate into the regulated banking system, threatening overall financial stability in ways that current regulatory frameworks are ill-equipped to handle.

Implications

The report's findings carry significant implications for the future of financial regulation. The era of treating NBFIs as a peripheral concern appears to be over. The BIS implicitly calls for a paradigm shift towards more comprehensive, activity-based regulation that looks across the entire financial landscape, rather than focusing solely on traditional institutions. This would involve closing regulatory gaps by implementing consistent international standards for leverage and liquidity management within the non-bank sector. For artificial intelligence, the implications point toward an urgent need for a new supervisory toolkit. Regulators must develop the capacity to audit and validate complex algorithms, establish standards for model transparency and data governance, and conduct system-wide stress tests that specifically account for AI-driven concentration risks. The challenge will be to craft regulations that mitigate systemic threats without stifling the genuine innovation and efficiency gains that these new technologies promise.

For central banks, the review underscores the increasing complexity of their dual mandate: maintaining price stability while safeguarding financial stability. The divergence in monetary policy paths among major economies is already creating a volatile environment for exchange rates and capital flows, complicating the fight against inflation for many countries. The added risks from the NBFI and AI domains mean that central bankers can no longer rely solely on interest rates. They may need to more actively deploy macroprudential tools, such as countercyclical capital buffers or leverage limits targeted at the NBFI sector. Furthermore, the report raises difficult questions about the central bank's role as a lender of last resort. A crisis originating in the non-bank sector could force central banks to decide whether to extend emergency liquidity to these less-regulated entities to prevent systemic collapse, a move that would be fraught with moral hazard concerns.

Contrarian View

While the BIS report paints a cautious picture, some analysts argue the focus on AI and NBFI risks may be premature. They contend that AI-driven efficiency gains could significantly bolster financial institutions' resilience, making them better at managing risk than human-led predecessors. Similarly, the growth of NBFIs could be viewed as a positive market evolution, providing much-needed credit to sectors underserved by traditional banks and dispersing risk away from the core banking system. From this perspective, heavy-handed regulation could stifle innovation and credit creation, proving more harmful than the risks it aims to prevent.

FAQ

  • What is the main message from the BIS September 2025 review? The global financial system is at an inflection point, facing novel risks from AI and the largely unregulated non-bank financial sector, all while navigating a complex macroeconomic environment of divergent monetary policies and geopolitical fragmentation.
  • Why is the BIS so concerned about non-bank financial intermediaries (NBFIs)? The BIS is concerned because NBFIs have grown to systemic importance in credit markets but operate with less regulatory oversight, higher leverage, and potential liquidity mismatches compared to traditional banks. This creates vulnerabilities that could trigger or amplify a financial crisis.
  • Does the report suggest that AI is a net negative for the financial industry? Not necessarily. The report acknowledges AI's significant potential for boosting efficiency and productivity. However, it strongly emphasizes that these benefits come with new, complex systemic risks—such as model herding and opacity—that regulators and firms are not yet fully equipped to manage.
