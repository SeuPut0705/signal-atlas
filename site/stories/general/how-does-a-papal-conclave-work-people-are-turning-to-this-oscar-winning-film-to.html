TL;DR

Recent health concerns surrounding Pope Francis, including a hospitalization for a lung infection, have ignited global curiosity about the ancient and secretive process of electing a new pope. In an era of instant information, many are turning to an unlikely source for answers: the 2019 Oscar-winning Netflix film, 'The Two Popes.' This trend highlights a modern reliance on cinematic storytelling to decode complex historical traditions. The film, while a dramatization, offers a visually compelling entry point into the papal conclave, the sacred ritual where cardinals from around the world gather in the Sistine Chapel to choose the next leader of the Catholic Church. As the world watches the Vatican, the film provides a lens through which to understand the tradition, politics, and profound secrecy of this pivotal event.

Key Data Points

  • Pope Francis's recent hospitalization has renewed global speculation and intense interest in the protocols for papal succession.
  • The papal conclave is a centuries-old, highly secretive process where cardinals under the age of 80 are sequestered in the Vatican to elect a new pope.
  • The 2019 film 'The Two Popes' has experienced a surge in viewership as an accessible, albeit dramatized, explainer of the conclave's atmosphere and mechanics.
  • Key traditions of the conclave include the cardinals' oath of secrecy, multiple rounds of voting, and the iconic black and white smoke signals from the Sistine Chapel chimney.
  • A candidate must secure a two-thirds majority vote from the attending cardinals to be elected pope, a rule that ensures a broad consensus.
  • Pope Francis has significantly shaped the electorate, appointing a majority of the cardinals who will choose his successor, diversifying the group geographically and ideologically.

Deep Dive

The papal conclave, a term derived from the Latin 'cum clave' (meaning 'with a key'), is a process steeped in centuries of tradition designed to ensure secrecy and divine guidance. Following the death or resignation of a pope, the Dean of the College of Cardinals summons the world's cardinals to Rome. Only those under the age of 80 are eligible to vote. The electors are then completely sequestered from the outside world within Vatican City, a measure to prevent external political influence. The voting itself takes place within the hallowed walls of the Sistine Chapel, under Michelangelo's 'The Last Judgment.' In a series of ballots, up to four per day, each cardinal writes the name of their chosen candidate. After each vote, the ballots are tallied. If no candidate reaches the required two-thirds majority, the process is repeated.

The world learns of the conclave's progress through a simple, yet iconic, method: smoke signals. After an unsuccessful vote, the ballots are burned along with a chemical agent to produce black smoke, billowing from a chimney atop the Sistine Chapel to inform the crowds in St. Peter's Square that a new pope has not yet been chosen. When a candidate finally achieves the necessary two-thirds majority and accepts the position, the ballots are burned alone, creating the famous white smoke that signals a successful election. Shortly after, the senior cardinal deacon appears on the central balcony of St. Peter's Basilica to announce 'Habemus Papam!' ('We have a pope!'), revealing the identity of the newly elected pontiff and the name he has chosen for his reign.

Implications

The public's turn toward 'The Two Popes' exemplifies a significant cultural shift where popular media serves as a primary educational tool for complex subjects. For many, the secretive and ritualistic nature of the Vatican is inaccessible. A well-produced film provides a narrative framework that makes the institution's traditions digestible and human. While the movie takes dramatic license, particularly with the imagined conversations between the future Pope Francis and Pope Benedict XVI, it successfully captures the visual grandeur, the political undercurrents, and the core mechanics of the election. This phenomenon demonstrates that cinematic storytelling can be more powerful than encyclopedic articles or documentaries in engaging a mass audience, effectively becoming a form of modern catechism for understanding opaque historical institutions.

A future conclave will be fundamentally shaped by the legacy of Pope Francis. He has appointed a significant majority of the cardinal-electors, intentionally diversifying the College of Cardinals away from its historical European, and particularly Italian, dominance. His appointees hail from developing nations and global 'peripheries,' bringing a wider range of pastoral experiences and theological viewpoints to the table. This 'Francis effect' makes the outcome of the next election far less predictable. The ideological tensions between conservative and progressive wings of the Church, hinted at in 'The Two Popes,' are now embodied by a far more geographically and culturally diverse group of electors. The next pope will be chosen by a body that reflects Francis's vision of a global church, which could lead to a successor who continues his pastoral path or a figure who represents a reaction to it.

Contrarian View

While 'The Two Popes' offers a compelling and visually rich entry point, relying on it as a definitive guide to the conclave is a mistake. The film is a biographical drama, not a documentary, and its primary focus is the fictionalized relationship and ideological dialogue between two men. It takes significant artistic liberties to create a compelling narrative, condensing complex theological debates and political maneuvering into palatable conversations. The real conclave is a far more intricate and less personal process, involving shifting alliances, bloc voting, and intense, private prayer and politicking that a feature film can only gesture towards. Over-reliance on such dramatizations risks fostering a superficial understanding that overlooks the deep spiritual gravity and complex geopolitical realities of electing a leader for 1.3 billion Catholics.

FAQ

  • What does the word 'conclave' mean? The word 'conclave' comes from the Latin phrase 'cum clave,' which translates to 'with a key.' It refers to the historical practice of locking the voting cardinals inside the Vatican to ensure they are shielded from outside influence and to encourage a swift election.
  • Do the cardinals talk to each other during the conclave? Yes, while there is a strict oath of secrecy regarding the proceedings, cardinals can and do discuss potential candidates and the needs of the Church among themselves between voting sessions. These discussions are a crucial part of the consensus-building process.
  • Can any Catholic be elected pope? Theoretically, any baptized male Catholic is eligible for election as pope. However, by long-standing tradition, the pope is chosen from within the College of Cardinals. The last time a non-cardinal was elected was in 1378.
  • How accurate is the depiction of the conclave in 'The Two Popes'? The film accurately portrays the physical setting (using a detailed replica of the Sistine Chapel), the voting process with ballots, and the use of black and white smoke signals. However, the central plot and the detailed private conversations between the future popes are a fictionalized dramatization for narrative purposes.
